Dynamic Lighting Controls for Greenhouses
Pulse Width Modulation, Algorithmic & Binary Control

Supplemental lighting is a major cost for year-round greenhouse producers in northern climates. Therefore, increasing efficiency of supplemental lighting is of primary concern to growers. Much research has focused on plant growth and morphology effects in response to spectral quality under sole source lighting. However, in greenhouse production, where natural light is supplemented with electric light, solar radiation provides bandwidths not present in electric light, minimizing the overall impact of precise spectral control on yield. It follows that any reduction in the quantity of supplemental light, while maintaining a minimum threshold photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD), will coincide with reduced electricity costs for growers. Modulating the intensity of supplemental light can be accomplished at high temporal resolution using computational control mechanisms (van Iersel & Gianino, 2017). The current research compares the efficacy of two lighting control programs, pulse width modulation (PWM) and binary off/on. PWM control modulates supplemental PPFD, balancing supplemental against ambient light in real time. Continuous modulation of PPFD is expected to precisely meet a minimum threshold while minimizing over lighting (wasted electricity). Binary control is expected to accurately provide a target daily light integral (DLI), with overshoot on cloudy days that become sunny. An adapted Light and Shade System Implementation (LASSI) serves as the core lighting control program in both the control and experimental conditions, while PWM and binary on/off features differ between conditions (Albright et al., 2000). In ongoing greenhouse experiments with leafy greens networked microcontrollers manage LumiGrow 650e LED fixtures. The project lays the groundwork for incorporation of additional software or open-source hardware into dynamic greenhouse systems.

The lighting control program strives to meet a target daily light integral (DLI). A quantum light sensor measures instantaneous photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD), which is a mix of electric light and sunlight. Power to an 650 watt LED is modulated, effectively dimming the light to precisely meet an instantaneous PPFD threshold. Each second, the program decides whether to turn the lights on or off based on the current DLI, taking into consideration the expected sum of sunlight to the current time, the potential for supplemental lighting until next sunrise, the season and the time of day. Where possible, the program defers supplemental lighting to take advantage of off-peak electricity rates. The DLI target is set at 17 mols, which is the crop-specific optimum for lettuce. DLI, PPFD, and power consumption are logged and wirelessly transmitted to the cloud directly from the greenhouse growing environment.

Historical Data

Global horizontal irradiance (GHI) data from the National Solar Resource Database was used to develop and test the control program. Years tested were 1999-2015, as well as typical meteorological year (TMY) data. Irradiance was converted to micromoles and integrated on a half-hour timestep. The test data informed subsequent adjustments to the rules by revealing precisely when each rule of the algorithm was applied. On days when the daily light integral (DLI) was below the target, the control program supplements solar radiation with electric light. The DLI target manifests in the adjusted data as a horizontal line. Although in this case the algorithm was only applied to lighting, similar rules have been applied to shading equipment, adjusting both up and down to meet the DLI target. The program was active in the late fall, winter, and early spring months, and less so in the summer, when solar radiation exceeded the instantaneous threshold required to meet the daily light integral.

Algorithm Rules
Albright, L. D., Both, A. J., & Chiu, A. J. (2000). Controlling greenhouse light to a consistent daily integral. Transactions of the ASAE, 43 (2), 421.

  • Rule 0

    Lights on.

  • Rule 1

    If time clock control is included and the current hour is during the period when lamps should be off, control is activated and the lamps are turned off.

  • Rule 2a

    For months of greatest solar irradiation, keep lamps off between sunrise and 12 hours after sunrise. However, if the daily accumulated PPFD is not equal to at least one-quarter of the daily target by solar noon, permit lights to remain on.

  • Rule 2b

    For late summer (when days are still sunny, but solar intensity has lessened), keep lamps off between sunrise and 9 hours after sunrise. However, if the daily accumulated PPFD is not equal to at least one-quarter of the daily target by solar noon, permit lights to remain on.

  • Rule 2c

    For spring and autumn months, keep lamps off between sunrise and 7 hours after sunrise.

  • Rule 2d

    For the rest of the months of the year, keep lamps off between sunrise and 2 hours after sunrise.

  • Rule 3

    If solar PPFD accumulated to this hour meets or exceeds the accumulation target (eq. 6) for the hour, turn the lights off.

  • Rule 4

    If the hour is during the time of year with more sunlight and between sunrise and sunset, and the PPFD left to be accumulated can be achieved by delaying supplemental lighting until the next hour even if solar PPFD drops suddenly to insignificance, and the PPFD deficit to this point could be made up by a scaled portion of the off-peak PPFD potential, turn off the lights.

  • Rule 5

    Turn off the lights if the hour is between sunrise and sunset and the PPFD left to be accumulated could be accumulated by turning on the lights at the next hour even if the solar PPFD drops immediately to insignificance and remains there for the rest of the day.

  • Rule 6

    If the hour is at sunset or between sunset and an hour before the start of off-peak electric rates and the accumulated PPFD deficiency to this hour could be achieved during off-peak hours alone, turn off the lights.

  • Rule 7

    If the hour is before off-peak electric rates start, but any remaining PPFD to be added by supplemental lighting will be achieved before the off-peak period ends, turn off the lights.

  • Rule 8

    Be sure lights are not turned off if the hour is during the dark part of the year and there remains more integrated PPFD to be added than can be met by the lamps, alone, operating from the next hour until the following sunrise.
